Fermat's 'little' theorem

Sadly I have to rush this one (anyone who cares to follow this up may do so by consulting my web site for my substantial Maple lecture Fermat's little theorem , introduced in Austria in 2001, marking the four hundreth anniversary of the birth of Pierre de Fermat (1601-1665), the first great number theorist since classical times).

Fermat's ' little ' theorem - a fundamental result in Number Theory - states that:

  • if p is any prime number,
  • and a is any integer not divisible by p ,
  • then leaves remainder 1 on division by p

Example #1. Let p = 7 and a = 2, then = 64 = 7 *9 + 1 .

 Example #2. Let p = 7 and a = 9, then = 531441 = 7 *75920 + 1 .

 Example #3. Let p = 17 and a = 9, then = 1853020188851841 = 17 *109001187579520 + 1 .

 Example #4. Let p = 27 and a = 2, then = 67108864 = 27 *2485513 + 13 .

Of course I'm hoping you may be thinking something like:

Oh! The remainder is not 1, rather it is 13; what's gone wrong ... ?

Nothing, you may also have noticed, has 'gone wrong'; it's simply that the '27' is itself not prime (it is 'composite', having at least one factor other than 1 and 27, the so-called 'trivial' factors of 27), and thus fails a requirement of Fermat's ' little ' theorem.

Examples with larger primes.

> p := ithprime(1000); # this gives the 1000-th prime
a := 2; a&^(p-1) mod p;

> p := ithprime(1000); # the same prime
a := 3; a&^(p-1) mod p;

> p := ithprime(1000); # and again
a := 22; a&^(p-1) mod p;

> p := ithprime(1000); # and again
a := 12345; a&^(p-1) mod p;

But see, for example, this:

> p := ithprime(1000); # this gives the 1000-th prime
a := 15838; a&^(p-1) mod p;

I hope you spotted that the remainder was not '1', and I hope also you can see a reason for that happening; it's simply that the value of ' a ' (namely 15838) is divisible by ' p ', and so fails a requirement of Fermat's theorem.
