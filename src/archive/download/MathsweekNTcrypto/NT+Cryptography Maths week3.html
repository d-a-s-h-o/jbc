Structure of my talk

The great Hungarian-USA mathematician Paul Halmos once wrote something to this effect: when you are giving a once-off talk this is what you should do:

  • Tell your audience what you are going to tell them
  • Tell your audience what you told them you were going to tell them
  • Tell your audience that you have told them what you told them you were going to tell them

What I am going to tell you

I am going to tell you a little bit - and in the time available it can only be a little bit - about:

  • 'Number Theory', which is, along with 'Geometry' the oldest branch of Mathematics. It is a vast subject, probably the most beautiful part of all of Mathematics, and it is - like all parts of the mathematical firmament - in a state of inexorable growth...
  • 'Cryptography', the science/art of confidential communication
  • Number Theory and Cryptography: a small intersection of those worlds...

I divide my talk into three sections:

1. Number Theory: a very, very, very, ... , very brief introduction... Here I have to avoid the temptation to start talking about Number Theory in general, and rather - because of time constraints - simply romp through three number theoretical topics (unfortunately having to ignore history , motivation , ... ) that play a central role in cryptographic applications.

Blandly stated (and it pains me having to do this, but time is the enemy...), those three topics are as follows:

ONE. Modular exponentiation

 TWO. The Euclidean algorithm

THREE. Fermat's ' little ' theorem

2. Cryptography: a very, very brief introduction

3. Number Theory and Cryptography: a brief glimpse at how some fundamental notions from 'elementary number theory' - all briefly treated in the Number Theory section - are used to create one very specific cryptographic method, the so-called Pohlig-Hellman private-key method.

Note. There is a remarkable, indeed revolutionary method, the so-called Rivest-Shamir-Adelman public-key method (the RSA method) that is similar to the Pohlig-Hellman method, but is fundamentally different... Time will not permit me to deal with it today, and I refer an interested reader to my Bill Clinton, Bertie Ahern, and digital signatures talk.
