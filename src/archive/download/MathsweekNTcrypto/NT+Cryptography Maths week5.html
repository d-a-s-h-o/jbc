Modular exponentiation

' Modular exponentiation ' is concerned with computations like these:

(a) What remainder does (that is 2 to the power of 5, namely 2*2*2*2*2, which is 32) leave when divided by 9?

(b) What remainder does (that is 7 to the power of 12, namely 7*7*7*7*7*7*7*7*7*7*7*7, which is 13841287201) leave when divided by 97?

(c) What remainder does (namely 4321*4321*4321*4321*... ... *4321*4321 (so there are 123 appearances of 4321)) leave when divided by 987654321?

Hand-performed computations. All of us can (I trust!) immediately provide the answer to (a): 9 divided into 32 'goes' 3 times, and leaves a remainder of 5:

32 = 9*3 + 5

Most people could provide - if pushed - an answer to (b): simply divide 97 into 13841287201, see how many times 'it goes', and see what the remainder is. Assuming you/I did it, we would find that:

13841287201 = 97*142693682 + 47

Thus the remainder that (namely 7 to the power of 12 ) leaves, on division by 97, is 47.

Computer-performed computation. While we could - in principle - provide an answer to question (c), it would certainly take a long time to do IF one was attempting to do it by hand, and in a naive way . Meaning?

Before I perform all three computations using Maple, I would like to introduce some notation; all the above computations are of the general form:

find/compute the remainder that leaves on division by m , where a , b , and m have given values

Now, three Maple matters:

#1 . Maple can divide one number into another and determine how many times 'it goes'.

To illustrate, I initially choose an example that you may do quickly in your head as you watch this

With m = 5 and a = 38, how many times does m divide into a ?

> a := 38; m := 5; iquo(a, m);

>

Thus 5 divided into 38 goes 7 times.

#2. And what is the remainder that the above a (38) leaves on division by the above m (5)?

That is determined by using the 'mod' command (there is another one - like 'iquo' - but I would rather not get diverted...) as follows:

> a := 38; m := 5; a mod m;

>

And the remainder is '3'. Thus, overall, 38 = 5*7 + 3:

> 5*7 + 3;

>

#3. Maple has a command for calculation ' to the power of '; it is ' ^ ' ('hat'; it is above the '6' on the keyboard)

Thus (namely 2 to the power of 3, also known as 2 cubed) - which you can do in your head (I hope!) is given by:

> 2^3;

Such computations may be carried out - using a (minor miracle) method known as the square-and-multiply technique - and for that method to be invoked in Maple one needs to use the ampersand (&), which is on the keyboard above the '7'.

Then, to calculate the remainder that leaves on division by m,

  • instead of using the command a^b mod m
  • one uses the command a&^b mod m
